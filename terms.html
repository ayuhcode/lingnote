Have you ever had a desire to be able to read a foreign sentences without learning that actual foreign language? Well one of the most powerful tools that linguists can use is Interlinear Glossing, also called as Leipzig Glossing.
The basic structure of Interlinear Glossing will include three main parts: The original sentence, the word-by-word/morpheme-by-morpheme gloss, and the translated sentence. In some cases, the original sentence will not be written in the standard Latin alphabet, so often linguists will add a conventional translation into the Latin alphabet for better understanding.

We are humans, the most intelligent creatures on Earth. That's why we have the ability to express hundreds of thousands ways in just a single sentence. And in linguistics, those ways are called as moods and cases.
Grammatical cases, in the simplest understanding, works like a "nametag" for words - to show what specific job they do in a sentence. Let's take these three examples, and focus on the word "dog":
The boy pets the dog
The dog runs
The dog's toy
In the first sentence, the dog is the one receiving the action pet, which we call as the object case. In the second sentence, we can see that now the dog is not receiving the action anymore, reversely he is the one that DOES the action, in a case so-called as subject case. And in the third sentence, the dog now neither does or receives the action, he is OWING the object - the toy; this is called the possessive case. So through these three sentences, the roles of the word "dog" is not the same.

Grammatical mood, on the other hand, denote the "feeling" or "attitude" that the speakers want to imply in a sentence. Back to our dog, let's take another three examples:
The dog is an animal
Pet the dog!
I wish the dog were here
In the first sentence, we simply just tell the fact - everyone knows that a dog is indeed an animal; this is called the indicative mood. Moving on to the second sentence, the listeners will clearly know that we are not in the same mood anymore, we are CALLING them, or, more precisely, give them the COMMANDS to do the action; this is called the imperative mood. And in the third sentence ... well, looks like the dog was running somewhere and was not here, and the listeners can imply that we are wanting to have the dog back; this is called as the subjunctive mood.
